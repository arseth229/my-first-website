About Borderline Personality Disorder

History

Borderline-Personality-Disorder or BPD for short was first noted by the American psychoanalyst Adolf Stern; however, it was not till 1980 that the disorder was actually published in the 3rd edition of the DSM thus making it a recognized diagnoses. It's name originates from earlier observations from people who were thought to exhibit the disorder. Describing it as being on the "border," of neurosis and psychosis, both of which are outdated now, where the patient seemed to dip into a kind of limbo between the two when exposed to certain forms of stress. The part which seemed to astonish the researchers was the rapid return to emotional stability once the stress had dissipated.

BPD Today

BPD continues to be a controversial topic in the mental health community as our understanding of it's basic nature seems to change with each study published. Many studies have been published over the years, each presenting new specific and general observational data, yet few if any have been able to be entirely conclusive due to cognitive impairments which seem to be prevalent in most if not all patients. Thus far the observed impairments include working memory processes, activities of planning and problem solving, set shifting, and decision making. In particular the imapirment of working memory has hindered progress as those diagnosed have a tendency to distort existing memories beyond the standard difference in perception and create false memories, in both instances it is common for symptoms to become exasperated due to the way they distort. Currently the only options for treatment are various talk therapies to help mitagate symptoms, but those appear to be ineffective long term as the recurrence rate continues to be 99% after 16 years.(which a majority of seems to stem from a lack of meaningful relationships later in life)
